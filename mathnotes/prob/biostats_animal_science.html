Probability and Counting

Mathematically, probability is $P = m/n$, where $m$ is the number of favorable trials, and $n$ is the number of trials in total. The probability of any simple event must be between $0$ and $1$, and the sum of probabilities of all simple events is equal to $1$.

An event consists of one or more simple/elementary events. The probability of an event $A$ is equal to the sum of probabilities of the simple events in event $A$.

Multiplicative Rule

Consider $k$ sets of elements of size $n_1, n_2, ..., n_k$. For example, $3$ pens with animals marked as listed:

  • Pen 1: 1,2,3
  • Pen 2: A,B,C
  • Pen 3: x, y

The possible triplets with one animal taken from each pen is $3 \times 3 \times 2 = 16$.

$3Ax$$3Ay$$3Bx$$3By$$3Cx$$3Cy$

Permutations

The number of ways $n$ elements can be arranged is the permutations of $n$ elements, and is equal to $P_n = n!$

Example:

In how many ways can 3 animals x, y and z be arranged in pairs such that the order in pairs is important?

$$P_{n,k} = \frac{3!}{(3-2)!} = 6$$

Combinations

From a set of $n$ elements, the number of ways these $n$ elements can be taken $k$ at a time regardless of order is:

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{k! (n-k)!} = \frac{n(n-1)...(n-k+1)}{k!}$$

Example:

In how many ways can 3 animals x, y, and z be arranged in pairs when the order in pairs is not important?

$$\binom{n}{k} = \frac{3}{2} = \frac{3!}{2! (3-2)!} = 3$$

$xy$
$xz$
$yz$

Partition Rule

From a set of $n$ elements to be assigned $j$ groups of size $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_j$, the number of ways in which these elements can be assigned is:

$$\frac{n!}{n_1!, n_2!, \ldots, n_j!}, \text{ where } n=n_1 + n_2 + \ldots, + n_j$$

Example:

In how many ways can a set of five animals be assigned to $j = 3$ stalls with $n_1 = 2$ animals in the first, $n_2 = 2$ animals in the second, and $n_3 = 1$ animal in the third?

$$\frac{5!}{2!2!1!} = 30$$

Conditional Probability

Compound Events

A compound event is composed of two or more events. The compound event such that both events $A$ and $B$ occur is called the intersection of events, denoted $A \cap B$. The compound event such that either event $A$ or $B$ occurs is called the union of events, denoted $A \cup B$. The complement of event $A$ is the event that $A$ does not occur, and is denoted by $A^c$.

A conditional probability is the probability that an event will occur if some assumptions are satisfied. i.e., the probability that an event $B$ will occur if it is known that event $A$ has already occurred.

$$P(B|A) = \frac{A \cap B}{P(A)}$$

When events are independent, the probability that both events occur is equal to the product of each probability

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$$

An example of independent events: we throw a dice two times. What is the probability of obtaining two sixes?

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B) = \frac{1}{6} \times \frac{1}{6} = \frac{1}{36}$$

Example:

From a deck of 52 playing cards we draw 2. What is the probability that both cards drawn are aces?

In a deck there are 4 aces; the probability that both cards drawn are aces is $P(A \cap B)$. The events are dependent; the drawing of the seconds card depends on which card has been drawn first.

First Draw: $P(A = ace) = \frac{4}{52} = \frac{1}{13}$

Second Draw: $P(B = ace | A = ace) = \frac{3}{51}$

Thus:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B|A) = \frac{4}{52} \cdot \frac{3}{51} = \frac{1}{221}$$

Example:

There are 10 calves in a pen: 2 black, 3 red, and 5 spotted. They are let out one at a time in completely random order. Find the following probabilities:

a) that the first calf is spotted

$$P_{spotted} = \frac{5}{10} = \frac{1}{2}$$

b) the first calf is either black or red

$$P(black \cup red) = 1 - P_{spotted} = \frac{1}{2}$$

c) the second calf is black if the first was spotted

The number of black calves (2) divided by the number of calves remaining after removing a spotted one (9)

$$P(black | spotted) = \frac{2}{9}$$

d) the first calf is spotted and the second black

$$P[spotted \cap (black|spotted)] = \frac{5}{10} \frac{2}{9} = \frac{1}{9}$$

Bayes Theorem

Bayes theorem is useful for stating the probability of some event $A$ if there is information about the probability of some event $E$ that happened after the event $A$.

Consider two cages, $k_1$ and $k_2$. In the first there are $2$ brown and $1$ white mice. In the second there are $2$ brown and $2$ white mice.

Cage $k_1 = B, B, W$

Cage $k_2 = B, B, W, W$

A cage is randomly chosen and then a mouse is randomly chosen from the cage. If the chosen mouse is brown, what is the probability is is from the first cage?

The probability of choosing the first cage is $\frac{1}{2}$. In the second step, the probability of choosing a brown mouse from the first cage is $\frac{2}{3}$.

$$P(K_1 \cap B) = P(K_1) P(B|K_1) = \frac{1}{2} \times \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}$$

Probability Distributions For Discrete Random Variables

Example:

Calculate the expectation and variance of the number of heads resulting from tossing $2$ coins.

$$E(y) = \mu \sum_i p(y_i) y_i = \frac{1}{4} (0) + \frac{1}{2}(1) + \frac{1}{4}(2) = 1$$

The expected value is $1$ head and $1$ tails when tossing two coins

Bernoulli Distribution

A Bernoulli distribution considers a binary random variable that can only take two values. The probability distribution of the Bernoulli distribution is:

$$p(y) = p^y q^{1-y} \text{, for } y = {0,1}$$

Thus:

$$p(y_1 = 1) = p$$

$$q(y_1 = 0) = q$$

The expectation and variance of a Bernoulli distribution are:

$$E(y) = \mu = p$$

$$\sigma^2 = Var(y) = \sigma^2 = pq$$

Binomial Distribution

A binomial variable $y$ is the number of successes in $n$ Bernoulli trials. Characteristics of a binomial experiment are:

  1. The experiment consists of $n$ equivalent trials, independent of each other
  2. There are only two possible outcomes of a single trial, yes and no, or $1$ and $0$
  3. The probability of 'success' is the same from trial to trial
  4. The random variable $y$ is the number of successes in the total of $n$ trials

$$p(y) = \binom{n}{y} p^y q^{n-y} \text{, where } y = 0, 1, 2, ..., n$$

  • $p$ is the probability of success in a single trial $q = 1-p$

The binomial distribution is symmetric only when $p=0.5$, and asymmetric in all other cases. The expectation and variance of a binomial variable are:

$$E(y) = \mu = np$$

$$Var(y) = \sigma^2 = npq$$

Example:

Determine the probability distribution of the number of female calves in $3$ consecutive calvings. Assume that only a single calf is possible of each calving, and that probability of a female is $0.5$.

Possible outcomes are y = 0, 1, 2, 3

  • $y=0 \to \binom{3}{0} (0.5)^0 (0.5)^3 = 0.125$
  • $y=1 \to \binom{3}{1} (0.5)^1 (0.5)^2 = 0.375$
  • $y=2 \to \binom{3}{2} (0.5)^2 (0.5)^1 = 0.375$
  • $y=3 \to \binom{3}{3} (0.5)^3 (0.5)^0 = 0.125$
Example:

Swine population susceptability to a disease is genetically determined at a single locus. This gene has two alleles, $B$ and $b$. The disease is associated with the recessive allele $b$. Animals with the genotype $bb$ will have the disease, while $Bb$ will be carriers.

The frequency of the $b$ allele is equal to $0.5$. If a boar and a sow both with $Bb$ genotypes are mated and produce a litter of $10$ piglets:

a) How many piglets are expected to have the disease?

$$np = 2.5$$

b) What is the probability that none of the piglets have the disease?

$$P(y=0) = \binom{10}{0} p^0 q^{10} = 1(0.25)^0 (0.75)^{10} = 0.056$$

c) What is the probability that at least one piglet has the disease?

$$P(y \ge 1) = 1 - P(y=0) = 1 - 0.056 = 0.944$$

d) What is the probability that half the litter has the disease?

$$P(y=5) = \binom{10}{5} p^5 q^5 = \frac{10!}{5!5!} (0.25)^5 (0.75)^5 = 0.058$$

Example:

How many calves must a cow produce such that the probability of having at least one male calf is greater than 0.99?

$$P(y \ge 1) = 0.99$$

$$P(y \ge 1) = 1 - P(y \lt 1) = 1 - P(y=0) = 1 - \binom{n}{0} \frac{1}{2}^0 \frac{1}{2}^n = \frac{a^n}{2}$$

$n = 6.64$, which gets rounded to $7$

Hyper-Geometric Distribution

Assume a set of size $N$ with $R$ successes and $N-R$ failures. A single trial has only two outcomes, but the set is finite, and each trial depends on the outcomes of previous trials. The random variable $y$ is the number of successes in a sample of size $n$ drawn from the source set of size $N$. Such a variable has a hypergeometric distribution.

$$P(y) = \frac{ \binom{R}{y} \binom{N-R}{n-y} }{ \binom{N}{n} }$$

  • $N$ is the size of the source set, $n$ the size of the sample
  • $R$ is the number of successful trials in the source set
  • $N-R$ is the number of failures in the source set
  • $n-y$ is the number of failures in the sample
  • $y$ is the random variable, the number of successes in the trial

Properties of the hyper-geometric distribution are that:

  • $n \lt N$
  • $0 \lt y \lt min(R,n)$

The expectation and variance are:

$$E(y) = \mu = \frac{nR}{N}$$

$$Var(y) = \sigma^2 = \frac{nR(N-R)}{N^2} \left( 1 - \frac{n-1}{N-1} \right)$$

Example:

In a box there are $12$ male and $6$ female piglets. If $6$ are chosen at random, what is the probability of getting $5$ males and $1$ female?

$$P(y) = \frac{ \binom{R}{y} \binom{N-R}{n-y} }{ \binom{N}{n} } = \frac{ \binom{12}{5} \binom{6}{1} }{ \binom{18}{6} } = 0.2559$$

Poisson Distribution

The Poisson distribution is a model for the relative frequency of rare events and data defined as counts. It is often used to determine the probability that some event will happen in a specific time, volume, or area. A Poisson random variable $y$ is defined as how many time some event occurs in a specific time, volume, or area. The probability that some event will occur is equal for any part of time (etc.) and the expected number of events is $\lambda$.

$$P(y) = \frac{ e^{-\lambda} \lambda^y }{ y! }$$

When $p$ is small and the total number of trials ($n$) is large, the binomial distribution can be approximated with a Poisson distribution. Both the expectation and the variance are equal to $\lambda$.

$$E(y) = \mu \lambda$$

$$Var(y) = \sigma^2 = \lambda$$

Example:

In a population of mice, 2% have cancer. In a sample of 100, what is the probability that more than 1 has cancer?

$$P(y) = \frac{ e^{-2} 2^y }{ y! }$$

$$P(y \gt 1) = 1 - P(y=0) - P(y=1) = 1 - 0.1355 - 0.27906 = 0.5941$$

Multinomial Distribution

The multimodal distribution is a generalization of the binomial distribution, in which there can be more than 2 outcomes (not just a single binary). There are $k$ possible outcomes of a single trial, each with its own probability. Single trials are independent. The probability mass function is:

$$p(y_1, y_2, \ldots, y_k) = \frac{n!}{y_1! y_2! \ldots y_k!} p_1^{y_1} p_2^{y_2} \ldots p_k^{y_k}$$

The number of occurrences $y_i$ of an outcome $i$ has expectation and variance:

$$E(y_1) = u_i = np_i$$

$$Var(y_1) = \sigma^2 = np_i(1-p_i)$$

Example:

Calving ease is defined in 3 categories labelled 1, 2, and 3. The probabilities for a single calving to be in 1, 2, and 3 are $0.6$, $0.3$, and $0.1$ respectively. What is the probability that out of $10$ cows, $8$ are in the first category, $1$ is in the second, and $1$ is in the third?

  • $n = 10$
  • $p_1 = 0.6$
  • $p_2 = 0.3$
  • $p_3 = 0.1$
  • $y_1 = 8$
  • $y_2 = 1$
  • $y_3 = 1$

$$p(y_1, y_2, y_3) = \frac{n!}{y_1! y_2! y_3!} p_1^{y_1} p_2^{y_2} p_3^{y_3}$$

$$p(8,1,1) = \frac{10!}{8! 1! 1!} (0.6)^8 (0.3)^1 (0.1)^1 = 0.045$$

Continuous Probability Distributions

The properties of a continuous variable are:

  1. The cumulative distribution $F(y)$ is continuous
  2. The random variable $y$ has an infinite number of values
  3. The probability that $y$ has a particular equals $0$

The density function is the first derivative of the cumulative distribution function. The cumulative distribution function is an integral of the function representing the area under the density for the interval $[\infty,y]$.

$$F(y_0) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(y)dy$$

A function is a density function if it satisfies the following properties:

$$f(y_i) \ge 0$$

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(y) ~dy = 1$$

The probability that $y$ is any value between $y_1$ and $y_2$ is:

$$P(y_1 \le y \le y_2) = \int_{y_1}^{y_2} f(y)dy$$

The expectation of a continuous random variable y is

$$E(y) = \mu_y = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} yf(y) ~dy$$

The variance of a continuous variable is:

$$Var(y) = \sigma_y^2 = E[(y-\mu)^2] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (y-\mu_y)^2 f(y)dy$$

The Uniform Distribution

The uniform variable $y$ is a variable that has the same probability for any value $y$, in an interval ($a$ to $b$). The density function is:

\[ f(y) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{b-a} \text{, if } a \le y \le b; \\ 0 \text{, for all other y}. \end{cases} \]

The expectation and variance are:

$$E(y) = \mu = \frac{a+b}{2}$$

$$Var(y) = \sigma^2 = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$$

Normal Distribution

The PDF of the normal distribution is:

$$f(y) = \frac{1}{ \sqrt{2 \pi \sigma^2} } exp((-y-u)^2 / 2 \sigma^2)$$

The coefficient of skewness and kurtosis is equal to zero

$$sk = E \left[ \left( \frac{y-\mu}{\sigma} \right)^3 \right] = 0$$

$$ku = E \left[ \left( \frac{y-\mu}{\sigma} \right)^4 \right] - 3 = 0$$

Example:

Assume a normal distribution of calf weights at age 6 months defined with $\mu = 200kg$ and $\sigma = 20kg$. Determine the theoretical proportions of calves that are...

a) More than $230kg$

$$z_0 = \frac{230-200}{20} = 1.5$$

$230kg$ is $1.5$ standard deviations beyond the mean

$$P(z \gt 1.5) = 0.0668$$

The percentage of calves expected to be heavier than $230kg$ is $6.68\%$

b) Less than $ 230kg$

$$1 - 0.0668 = 0.9332$$

c) Less than $210kg$ and more than $170kg$

$$y_1 = 170kg$$

$$y_2 = 210kg$$

$$z_1 = \frac{170-200}{20} = -1.5$$

$$z_2 = \frac{210-200}{20} = 0.5$$

We find the probability that the variable takes values between -1.5 and 0.5 standard deviations from the mean:

$$P(z \gt 1.5) = 0.0668$$

$$P(z \gt 0.5) = 0.3085$$

$$P(-1.5 \le z \le 0.5) = 1 - [P(z \gt 1.5) + P(z \gt 0.5)] = 1 - (0.0668 + 0.3085) = 0.6247$$

$62.47%$ of calves are expected to have weights between $170kg$ and $210kg$

d) What is the theoretical lowest value for an animal to be included among the heaviest 20%?

This corresponds to the area under the standard normal curve for values greater than some value $z_0.$

$$P(z_0 \ge z \ge \inf) = 0.20$$

From the table, the value of $z_0$ is 0.84. Now it must be transformed to the original scale

$$z_0 = \frac{y_0 - \mu}{\sigma}$$

$$y_0 = \mu + z_0 \sigma$$

$$y_0 = 200 + 0.84(20) = 216.8kg$$

Animals greater than or equal to $216.8kg$ are expected to be among the heaviest $20\%$.

Multivariate Normal Distribution

Consider a set of n random variables, $[y_1, y_2,~...,~y_n]$, with means $[\mu_1, \mu_2,~...,~\mu_n]$, variance $[\sigma_1^2, \sigma_2^2,~...,~\sigma_n^2]$, and covariances among them $[\sigma_{12}, \sigma_{13},~...,~\sigma_{n-1}]$

This can be expressed as vectors and matrices as follows:

$$\mathbf{y} = \begin{bmatrix} y_1 \\ y_2 \\ ... \\ y_n \end{bmatrix} ~\mathbf{\mu} = \begin{bmatrix} \mu_1 \\ \mu_2 \\ ... \\ \mu_n \end{bmatrix} ~\mathbf{V} = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 \sigma_{12} ~...~ \sigma_{1n} \\ \sigma_{12} \sigma_2^2 ~...~ \sigma_{2n} \\ ...~~~...~... \\ \sigma_{1n} \sigma_{2n} ~...~ \sigma_n^2 \end{bmatrix}$$

$\mathbf{V}$ denotes the variance-covariance matrix of the vector $\mathbf{y}$. The vector $\mathbf{y}$ has a multivariate normal distribution if its probability density function is:

$$f(y) = \frac{exp(-1/2(y-\mu)'\mathbf{V}^{-1}(y-\mu))} {\sqrt{(2\pi)^n|\mathbf{V}|}}$$

where $|\mathbf{V}|$ denotes the determinant of $\mathbf{V}$.

Chi-Square Distribution

Consider a set of standard normal variables $z_i$, where $i = 1, ..., z$, that are IID with mean $\mu=0$ and standard deviation $\sigma=1$. Define a random variable:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_iz_i^2$$

$$~text{for}~ i = 1, ... \nu~\text{degrees of freedom}$$

The variable chi-squared has a chi-square distribution with nu degrees of freedom (the shape depends on the degrees of freedom. The expectation and variance of a chi-squared variable are:

$$E[\chi^2] = \nu$$

$$Var[\chi^2] = 2\nu$$

The mean of a standard normal variable equals zero, and in this case the chi-square distribution is ‘central’. A noncentral chi-square distribution is:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_iy_i$$

$$~for~ i = 1, ... \nu~\text{degrees of freedom}$$

where $y$ is a normal variable with mean $\mu_i$ and variance $\sigma^2 = 1$. The distribution is defined by degrees of freedom and the non-centrality parameter, $\lambda$.

$$\lambda = \sum_i\mu_i^2$$

$$~for~ i = 1, ... \nu~\text{degrees of freedom}$$

The expectation of the noncentral $\chi_{\nu}^2$ variable is:

$$E[\chi^2] = \nu + \lambda$$

With the non-central distribution, the mean is shifted right.

Student’s t-Distribution

Let $z$ be a standard normal variable with $\mu = 0$ and $\sigma = 1$, and let $\chi^2$ be a chi-square random variable with nu degrees of freedom. Then,

$$t = \frac{z} {\sqrt{\chi^2\text{/}\nu}}$$

By decreasing the degrees of freedom, the curve becomes flatter in the middle and heavier in the tails. The expectation and variance of the $t$ variable are:

$$E[t] = 0$$

$$Var[t] = \frac{\nu}{\nu-2}$$

This is called a central t-distribution, because the numerator of the t variable is a standard normal variable. A non-central t-distribution is a distribution of:

$$t = \frac{y} {\sqrt{\chi^2\text{/}\nu}}$$

  • where $y$ is a normal variable with mean of $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$ of 1

Again, the non-centrality parameter is $\lambda$, which determines at which point the distribution is centered.

F-Distribution

Let $\chi_1^2$ and $\chi_2^2$ be two independent chi-square random variables with $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$ degrees of freedom respectively. Then,

$$F = \frac{\chi_1^2\text{/}\nu_1}{\chi_2^2\text{/}\nu_2}$$

is a random variable with degrees of freedom $\nu_1$ and $\nu_2$. The shape of the F distribution depends on the degrees of freedom. The expectation and variance of the non-central $F$ variable is:

$$E(F) = \frac{\nu_2}{\nu_2-2}\left(1 + \frac{2\lambda}{\nu_1}\right)$$

The expectation and variance of the centered F-distribution is:

$$E(F) = \frac{\nu_2}{\nu_2-2}$$

$$Var(F) = \frac{2\nu_2^2(\nu_1 + \nu_2 - 2)}{\nu_1(\nu_2-2)^2(\nu_2-4)}$$

Again, a non-centrality parameter $\lambda$ shifts the center of the distribution to the right as it increases.
